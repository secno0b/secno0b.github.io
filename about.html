About

Kaushik Joshi

I started my career hitting F12 on Windows XP machines to PXE boot them through migrations to Windows 7 and 8. No security title, no architecture diagrams. Just a room full of hardware that needed to move forward.

From there I helped stand up the IT infrastructure for the Olivia Newton-John Cancer Wellness and Research Centre in Melbourne. Nine floors of equipment. Breaking open boxes, racking gear, plugging in printers, cabling rooms. The kind of work where you learn how systems actually get built, not how they look in a proposal.

That led to a desktop support role, and then into SOE (Standard Operating Environment) engineering at a managed services company. I was building custom Windows images for large Australian enterprises like Toyota and Medibank, learning what it means to deliver consistent, repeatable infrastructure at scale.

I moved into a hybrid role supporting both brownfield and greenfield projects for a private healthcare company, acting as L3/L4 escalation across their environment. That's where security found me. Around 2014, 2015, I was helping hospitals maintain security hygiene before most organizations were even thinking about it as a discipline. Patching, hardening, access controls, all driven by the reality that healthcare systems can't go down and can't afford to be compromised.

After that I joined Hansen Technologies, a role I landed through a connection at the gym who happened to be the hiring manager. Sometimes the best career moves start outside a recruiter's inbox.

Then came Caterpillar, where I've been since 2019. I lead security engineering and DevOps for Cat Digital, managing a team across application security, cloud security architecture, and DevOps. The portfolio spans 70+ applications and 288 repositories. The work sits in the gap between what security frameworks recommend and what production systems actually allow: policy enforcement that breaks integrations, dependency tooling that gives you an A while you're years behind, identity architectures spanning multiple tenants with conflicting auth models.

Along the way I've picked up CISM, TOGAF, Microsoft Cybersecurity Architect, and AWS Security Specialty certifications, among others. But the most useful things I've learned came from the work itself, not the exams.
